01. Fix a mess

Rebuilding the operational plumbing at The Central App

Marketing & Operations Manager · Remote from Central Otago · 2025–present

Central Otago's leading community news platform reaches over 800,000 monthly page views and 60,000 downloads. The audience side was strong. The systems behind it were held together by manual work and a couple of people's memories.

The situation

HubSpot data lived at the contact level, so there was no clean way to see a customer's full relationship with the business. Ads expired without visibility, which meant we often found out after the fact. The inventory tracker was an Excel sheet even long-timers found confusing. There was no reliable customer list, no documented onboarding or offboarding for advertisers, and reporters couldn't see how their own stories were performing.

Billing errors dripped through the month and got caught only when someone noticed.

What I did

Restructured the CRM and sales pipeline

Rebuilt HubSpot around companies rather than contacts, so every customer relationship is visible in one place. Set up a deals pipeline that reflects how the business actually sells.

Automated what was manual

Set up Broadstreet expiry automation so we know before ads lapse, not after. Built a monthly reconciliation between Xero and the internal inventory tracker to catch billing errors in one pass rather than chasing them through the month.

Made performance visible

Built Looker Studio dashboards for the editorial team, giving reporters a weekly view of how their stories are actually performing. Set up quarterly client ad reports so the sales team has a concrete touchpoint moment for each customer.

Documented the tacit knowledge

Wrote onboarding and offboarding SOPs for advertisers, plus archive and naming conventions across the shared drives, so nothing depends on one person's memory.

Brought AI into the daily workflow

Set up Gemini gems the team now uses daily: one for writing better meta titles and descriptions, another for turning PR releases into publishable news copy. Both get used on every article.

02. Build from nothing

A 12-month launch plan for a founder who doesn't speak marketing

Freelance strategy · Australian small-business services client · 2026

A founder-led business preparing to launch a new B2B service into the Australian small-business market. Strong vision, no marketer in the team, no plan yet. My brief was Phase 1 of a go-to-market strategy for the CEO to review.

The situation

The client came to me with a brain dump: what the product was, who it was for, ambitious client acquisition targets, and a rough sense of what "launch" should feel like. That was it. No positioning framework, no channel strategy, no timeline, no budget shape, and no marketer inside the business to shape the work.

The other real constraint: the CEO isn't a marketer and doesn't want to be. A plan that only made sense to another marketer would fail on delivery. The final document had to be structured enough for the team to execute against, and readable enough for a non-marketer CEO to sign off on with genuine understanding.

What I did

Structured the brain dump into a real plan

Took the raw material and turned it into a 12-month, three-phase launch spanning awareness and warm activation, growth and conversion, and ongoing compounding activity. Presented as a Gantt-style overview so the whole year is visible at a glance.

Chose channels for how the audience actually behaves

Recommended an interactive quiz over a static whitepaper because founders won't finish a whitepaper. Recommended problem-aware search terms over broad category ones because the category isn't yet one people search for by name. Recommended founder peer community speaking slots over cold outreach because the return on time is significantly higher. Every channel choice reflected who the audience is and how they behave.

Sequenced for compounding effect

SEO and organic content running quietly from day one, so it's paying off by mid-year when the launch-critical activities peak. Paid LinkedIn built on top of already-published organic content rather than boosting from cold. Google Ads timed to build toward the biggest lead generation event of the year, not run flat.

Wrote the plan for a non-marketer to defend

Included plain-language descriptions of every initiative in the plan itself. Not just what each activity is, but why it belongs in this phase, what it depends on, and what it's meant to produce. The CEO can now read the plan and understand the strategy without needing me in the room.

Made clear what wasn't in scope, and why

A software marketplace integration the client had raised as an early idea got flagged as a future goal rather than a Phase 2 activity, because it needs product development resource before marketing can do anything with it.

How I approach it

"A big part of the job on projects like this is translation. Non-marketer clients need to understand why the plan looks the way it does, not just what's in it. If they can't defend it back to their own team, it doesn't launch."
03. Deliver end to end

New Zealand's first Credit Risk and Fraud Summit

Freelance project + marketing · CR&F Summit 2026 · NZ credit risk client · 12-month build to launch

A New Zealand credit risk business wanted to establish the country's first national industry summit for credit risk and fraud specialists. My brief was end-to-end: build the brand, run the marketing programme, manage sponsors, learn the event tech, and coordinate with the external events management team.

The situation

Nothing existed yet. No brand, no site, no reputation, no template. The ambition was to establish a national industry event in a sector that had never had one, and to make it feel like a community moment rather than a company-branded conference.

That was the strategic tightrope. The client was funding and hosting the summit but deliberately didn't want to be branded as the host. The event needed to feel like an industry moment the community owned, not corporate marketing for the client. Every piece of copy, every LinkedIn post, every design decision had to hold that line.

What I did

Ran the marketing programme end to end

LinkedIn content calendar (46 posts over 12 months), website content and structure, EDM programme, sponsor communications, and post-event review. This was the marketing function for the event, delivered as one person.

Managed 13 sponsors across five tiers

Platinum through community-level sponsors, including onboarding, asset collection, LinkedIn coverage, on-site materials, and post-event reporting. Coordinated closely with the client's internal lead who owned relationships.

Learned EventsAir from scratch

The event ran on a specialist events platform I'd never used before. I picked it up, set up registrations, ran delegate communications, and had it operating smoothly by launch.

Partnered with a designer to build a cohesive brand

Worked with an external designer on a full identity system covering lanyards, presentation templates, wayfinding, exhibitor booth signage, registration desk, table cards, and event collateral. On-site brand consistency was one of the strongest pieces of delegate feedback.

Coordinated with the external events management team

Seven Events ran the physical event delivery. My role was to keep the marketing, digital, and brand streams aligned with theirs so nothing fell through the gaps between agencies.

Held the "host but not host" messaging line

Every LinkedIn post, every announcement, every piece of copy reinforced the summit as a community event. Community-first content ("Meet the Events Committee") outperformed everything else by a wide margin, which validated the strategic call.

"When the strategy works, it's invisible. Keeping the client out of the frame was the right call, and every piece of marketing had to protect it. You just look like a community, which was the whole point."
